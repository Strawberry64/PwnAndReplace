Password Security Tips


Don’t reuse your passwords

Using the same password for multiple accounts increases your vulnerability in case one of those accounts is compromised.

Don’t write your passwords down

Writing down passwords can lead to them being discovered by others. Instead, consider using a password manager.

Use a password manager

Password managers can generate and store complex passwords for you, reducing the risk of using weak or reused passwords.

Using a password manager

Password managers offer several advantages:

  • Convenience: Password managers can store and manage all your passwords, eliminating the need to remember multiple passwords.
  • Complex password generation: Password managers can generate strong, complex passwords for you.
  • Support for multiple devices: Password managers can sync your passwords across multiple devices.
  • Enhanced security: Password managers encrypt your passwords, making them more secure.

However, there are some drawbacks:

  • Single point of failure / dependency on software: If your password manager is compromised, all your passwords could be at risk.
  • Potential learning curve: Some password managers may have a learning curve for new users.
  • Cost: Some password managers require a subscription or purchase fee.
  • 2FA not required: While 2FA is a recommended security measure, it is not always required by password managers.

Don’t share your passwords

Sharing passwords, even with trusted individuals, can increase the risk of those passwords being compromised.

Use your keyboard as a canvas

Consider creating passwords using shapes or patterns of keys on your keyboard for added security.

Periodically update your passwords

Regularly changing your passwords, especially for critical accounts, can help protect your accounts from being compromised.

Use 2 factor authentication (2FA) or Single Sign On when possible

Implementing 2FA or SSO can add an extra layer of security to your accounts. SSO authenticates the user once and grants access to all authorized systems without the need for additional login prompts, while 2FA requires users to provide two or more authentication factors to verify their identity.

Use a phrase and incorporate shortcut codes or acronyms

Examples:

  • 2BorNot2B_ThatIsThe?
  • L8r_L8rNot2day
  • 4Score&7yrsAgo
  • John3:16=4G

Using a phrase that is meaningful to you and incorporating shortcut codes or acronyms can help create memorable and secure passwords.

Use passwords with common elements, but customized to specific sites

For example:

  • ABT2_uz_AMZ! (About to use Amazon)
  • ABT2_uz_BoA! (About to use Bank of America)
  • Pwrd4Acct-$$ (Password for account at the bank)
  • Pwrd4Acct-Fb (Password for a Facebook account)

Customizing passwords for different sites can help protect your accounts if one of your passwords is compromised.
